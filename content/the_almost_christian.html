"Uncovering the Dualism of Surface-Level Christianity: An Analysis of Inner Spiritual Transformation

Ye sought in vain from world to world, a rest from anxious care;
Ye found the world more false and cold, the trouble and the snare!
Ye still have sought the world around, and tired of all its toys;
Come, try the wisdom of the Lord, and taste immortal joys.

"Come, Try the Wisdom of the Lord"




John Wesley's sermon, The Almost Christian, is a masterful yet poignant analysis of the inner condition of a person's soul, divination from the surface level faith in God into the inner depths of spiritual metamorphosis. Although the sermon was delivered in 1741, its timeless message still resonates with people today and offers an astute reflection on the presence and power of an individual's spiritual core.

Wesley's sermons offer a unique insight into the internal motivations for faith by recognizing the dualism of surface-level Christianity, describing it as an outwardly- spiritual state but leaving "the inward evil" unobserved. This inner corruption can be resolved through a process of spiritual transformation, involving the introduction of gospel living into everyday life by "letting it penetrate your soul, by studying it every day, [and] by meditating on it eagerly." To truly become a Christian means to be obedient and faithful to God's will, synthesizing his Word into the fibre of one’s being.

With a sharp moral tone, Wesley makes clear that Christianity is about more than just belief. Without a commitment to faith and using His Word as the measure of one's life, it is impossible to reach the "true Christian" status. Put simply, an "almost Christian" is one who is only partly committed to the principles of Christianity and fails to evaluate themselves holistically by the words of the gospel.

Wesley argues that such people are easily deceived by their own works and lack of discipline, proclaiming "there is a wide difference between slighting the Scriptures and searching them." The understanding of the power and knowledge of the gospel allows one to truly encounter spiritual transformation. It is through “careful and serious” study that one is enabled to become “sincerely virtuous," thus living free of the chains of "worldly affections."

Ultimately, Wesley's sermon is designed to illustrate the power of true Christian conversion and its potential for internal transformation. By motivating people to seize their destiny and commit to the gospel, Wesley leaves his audience with a deeply spiritual message: seeking out the Word of God is a powerful act of spiritual awakening, one that allows individuals to reach a state of transformation and ultimately faith. Despite the passage of time, the power of The Almost Christian sermon and its message of internal transformation remains as timeless and relevant today as it did nearly 250 years ago.
